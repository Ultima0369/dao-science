# Translation Statement: On Untranslatability and Writing That Bears the Way

## 翻译声明：关于不可译性与文以载道

---

## I. A Fundamental Judgment

The bilingual support of this project is **not** an attempt to "translate" Chinese thought into English thought. That assumption itself commits the error warned against in `1_first_principles/03_map_not_territory.md`: mistaking the map for the territory.

Language, writing, image, and sound each have their own characteristics and limitations. The "six categories" of Chinese characters—pictographs, indicatives, associative compounds, phono-semantic compounds, transferred characters, and loan characters—carry a cognitive-historical sediment entirely different from alphabetic writing. A single Chinese character such as 道 (Dao) simultaneously resonates with road, speech, method, source, and process; no single English word—Way, Path, Principle, Logos—can exhaust it.

Therefore, the English version of this project is **not an equivalent replacement** of the Chinese version. It is:

> **Another map, built with English words, pointing to the same experiential territory.**

English readers should be explicitly told: when you encounter terms such as Dao, De, Wu Wei, Li Ru, Xing Ru, or Zhi Zhi—kept in their Chinese forms with glosses—you are not reading an English concept. You are being invited, through an English footnote, to return to the first-person experience to which the original Chinese term points.

---

## II. Five Principles for Handling Vocabulary

### 1. Keep the Chinese term + pinyin + brief gloss

The following terms are not forcibly translated; they are introduced with pinyin and an operational explanation at first appearance:

| Chinese | Pinyin | English handling | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| 道 | Dào | **Dao** | "Way" is too static, "Principle" too abstract, "Process" loses ontological resonance |
| 德 | Dé | **De** | "Virtue" carries moral judgment; De is closer to the precision of a generative model |
| 无为 | Wú Wéi | **Wu Wei** | "Non-action" is a misunderstanding; it means acting without opposing the direction of least action |
| 理入 | Lǐ Rù | **Li Ru** | No corresponding English contemplative category |
| 行入 | Xíng Rù | **Xing Ru** | Same as above |
| 知止 | Zhī Zhǐ | **Zhi Zhi** | "Knowing when to stop" loses its operational sense as a stopping criterion |
| 观 | Guān | **Guan** | "Contemplation" or "Observation" both lose the technical sense of evenly distributed precision |
| 相 | Xiàng | **Xiang** | "Appearance" is acceptable, but keeping Xiang in "Xiang is not the thing" avoids confusion with phenomenal content |
| 一 | Yī | **Yi / One** | "One" is acceptable, but must be clarified as unobstructed bandwidth, not the numeral |
| 报冤行 / 随缘行 / 无所求行 / 称法行 | Bào Yuān Xíng / Suí Yuán Xíng / Wú Suǒ Qiú Xíng / Chēng Fǎ Xíng | Keep pinyin, add English description when needed | The Four Practices are proprietary categories of the Bodhidharma tradition |

### 2. Translatable but annotated

| Chinese | English | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 觉知 | awareness | Not ordinary perception, but first-person clear presence |
| 心 | mind-heart | Not merely mind; includes affect and bodily feeling |
| 心性 | mind-nature | The structural disposition and plasticity of mind |
| 境界 | state / frame | Avoid mystification; refers to a temporary mode of mental organization |
| 功夫 | gongfu | Pinyin may be kept; refers to stable capacities formed by long-term training |

### 3. Scientific terms use standard English

Predictive coding, free energy, DMN, LTP, LTD, STDP, NMDA, and similar already have international English terms; use them directly without keeping the Chinese.

### 4. First-person experiential words keep their experiential index

Terms such as pain, clarity, dullness, scatteredness, tightness, and looseness should be translated so as to preserve their first-person verifiable quality, not merely to find conceptual synonyms.

### 5. Numbers, formulas, and diagrams are cross-linguistic

$$\text{Dao} \equiv -\nabla_\theta G(\pi_\theta)$$ needs no translation. It is the most honest language in the project.

## III. A Reminder for English Readers

> **When reading the English version of this project, treat every term kept in Chinese as an invitation, not a definition.**

Dao is not a synonym for "Way."
De is not "Virtue."
Wu Wei is not "Doing nothing."
Zhi Zhi is not "Stopping."

They are operational concepts grown in a different linguistic-historical soil. What the English version can do is tell you what experiences these terms point to, under what conditions they can be operated, and which neuroscience concepts they connect with. But real understanding can only happen in practice—just as you cannot learn to swim by reading a book about swimming.

---

## IV. Requirements for Translators

If you wish to contribute an English translation:

1. **Do not pursue one-to-one correspondence.** The goal of translation is not to give English readers "the same thing Chinese readers read," but to enable English readers to **arrive at the same experiential territory**.
2. **Keep key Chinese terms.** Do not sacrifice the irreducibility of concepts merely to make sentences "fluent."
3. **Prioritize operational instructions.** English readers most need to know: what to do, how to do it, what observations to expect, and when to stop.
4. **Philosophical exposition may be simplified but must not be distorted.** If a Chinese sentence has no English equivalent, keep the original Chinese with a note rather than substituting a different English sentence.
5. **Every translated file must link back to the Chinese version at the bottom** and clearly state "translation in progress."

---

## V. Relation to the Project's Core Framework

This translation statement is itself an extension of `03_map_not_territory.md`:

- Chinese is Map A;
- English is Map B;
- Both point to the same territory—the actual operation of the human mind;
- But neither map is the territory itself.

True "writing that bears the Way" does not ask writing to exhaust the Way, but asks writing to become a reliable finger pointing to the Way. Fingers may differ, but the moon is the same.
